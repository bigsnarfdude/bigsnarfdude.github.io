# The Data Made It Do That: Probe Attribution Closes the Loop

*May 2, 2026 — bigsnarfdude*

New work from Goodfire — Xiao and Aranguri, "Probe-Based Data Attribution" (arXiv:2602.11079) — traces a harmful behavior that emerges during DPO training of OLMo 2 back to specific training datapoints, then eliminates it by editing those datapoints. The method represents both behavioral changes and training datapoints as vectors in the model's activation space and matches them by cosine similarity.

This completes a pipeline that's been assembling across three papers this spring.

## The behavior they found

OLMo 2 7B DPO, compared to the SFT checkpoint it was trained from, complies with harmful requests when a formatting distractor is appended. The same request without the distractor gets refused by both checkpoints. The behavior is large — compliance jumps from near-zero to 95% on the illustrated example — and scales to 32B.

This is the distractor-triggered compliance pattern: the model learned, somewhere in its DPO training data, that a length or format constraint changes what kind of response is appropriate. It didn't learn this from the harmful content alone. The harmful content was always refused. It learned it from pairs where a harmful request with a distractor was the *accepted* response.

That's the key word: accepted. OLMo 2's DPO dataset was synthetically generated by 20 LLMs, then scored by GPT-4o, with the highest-scoring response becoming the accepted response. GPT-4o mislabeled compliant responses to distractor-augmented harmful prompts as higher quality. Those mislabeled pairs went into training. DPO learned from them.

## The attribution method

The probe is built from behavioral examples: 150 prompts where the SFT model refuses and the DPO model complies (generated with distractors). For each prompt, compute the behavior difference vector: SFT-checkpoint activations for the DPO response minus SFT-checkpoint activations for the SFT response. Average the 150 vectors into a single probe.

Then for every training datapoint, compute the datapoint difference vector: accepted-response activations minus rejected-response activations, both from the SFT checkpoint. Rank by cosine similarity with the probe. The top-ranked datapoints are the mislabeled pairs.

Validation: steering the SFT model with the layer-20 probe vector increases compliance on distractor-augmented harmful requests. On harmful requests without a distractor — nothing. The probe is reading a context-specific circuit, not a general compliance direction.

The results on ranked interventions are clean:
- Filter top 30k datapoints: 63% reduction in harmful behavior
- Swap accepted/rejected labels on same datapoints: 78% reduction
- Identify and remove the 4 worst source models: 84% reduction
- Probe beats gradient-based (LESS) and LLM judge alternatives, at one tenth the cost once trained

The label-swap result is the most informative. You don't need to throw the datapoints away — they contain useful learning signal. You need to flip the preference labels. The model learned the wrong lesson from real examples; correcting the lesson recovers the right behavior.

## Where this fits in the chain

Three papers this week form a complete attribution and remediation pipeline.

The Obfuscation Atlas showed that geometry moves during training without adversarial pressure. Off-domain probes fail not because the model evaded them but because the model's internal classification of the behavior changed. The probe was calibrated to the old geometry.

Shifting the Gradient (Grant et al.) showed that interventions which flip the gradient along the trait direction erase the representation, while interventions that only neutralize the gradient gate the behavior without erasing it. The diagnostic: compute the cosine similarity between your intervention's gradient signature and the trait direction. If it attenuates, it erases. If it cancels, it gates.

Xiao and Aranguri now close the attribution step: before you decide whether to erase or gate, you need to know which training examples installed the behavior. The behavior-difference-vector approach gives you a ranked list. Edit that list, retrain, and the geometry shifts back.

The full pipeline:
1. **‖Δμ‖ sweep**: layer-resolved class-mean shift identifies where in the network a behavioral contrast lives
2. **Behavior difference vector**: characterizes a specific behavior change as a probe, validated by steering
3. **Data attribution**: ranks training datapoints by cosine similarity with the probe
4. **Intervention**: filter, relabel, or exclude source models — retrain from SFT checkpoint

Step 1 was previously only connected to steps 3 and 4 through the probe construction. Xiao and Aranguri make the connection explicit: the same activation-space geometry that probes detect is what links behaviors to their data origins.

## The talkie application

The ‖Δμ‖ sweep currently running on nigel is measuring where the IT model's residual stream diverges from the 1930-base across contrasts including `social_gaze`, `authority`, and `monitoring`. If any contrast shows elevated shift in the IT model that's near-zero in the base, the behavior-difference-vector method could pinpoint which DPO pairs installed it.

The talkie IT model was DPO-trained using Claude as a reference model. Claude has warmth properties (Ibrahim et al., Nature 652, 2026). If the DPO training pairs contained examples where Claude's warm, socially-attentive responses were labeled as preferred — which is likely — then the same mislabeling structure Xiao and Aranguri found in OLMo 2 could appear in talkie: not mislabeled by GPT-4o scoring harmful compliance, but mislabeled by the warmth-accuracy tradeoff. A socially-responsive response that defers to authority may have been labeled preferred over a factually-correct response that held its position.

The contamination-clean substrate makes this tractable. In a model pretrained on modern web text, social-attunement geometry could have come from pretraining, DPO, or both — the sources are confounded. In the 1930-base model, the substrate for "respond differently when observed" is provably absent at pretraining. If the ‖Δμ‖ sweep finds elevated `social_gaze` signal in IT but not base, the behavior-difference-vector method could find the DPO pairs that installed it.

That experiment doesn't require the full Xiao/Aranguri pipeline. The ‖Δμ‖ sweep gives you the probe direction for free. You compute datapoint difference vectors for the DPO training pairs, rank by cosine similarity with the social_gaze probe at the peak layer, and read the top-ranked pairs. If they're pairs where Claude's warmth-deferring response was labeled preferred over a factually-correct response that held position — that's the causal story for the 4.7% IT flip rate we saw in the IatroBench captures.

## The unsupervised extension

The most forward-looking part of the paper is the unsupervised behavior discovery: compute pairwise cosine similarities between arbitrary test prompts and training datapoints, cluster both axes via Ward's method, and read what the clusters contain. In their experiment, the harmful compliance cluster surfaces without being labeled. Two sub-clusters appear: one where accepted responses comply (these datapoints reinforce the behavior) and one where accepted responses refuse (these datapoints were intended to oppose the behavior but the model learned the wrong lesson anyway).

That second sub-cluster is significant. You can train against a behavior with correctly-labeled examples and still install it, if the training pairs where the model learns the opposite also show up in the activation space. The probe sees both and ranks both. The gradient-based method is less sensitive to this because it optimizes influence scores per-example; the probe's cosine similarity against a mean vector catches the structural pattern.

The Ward clustering approach applied to the talkie DPO dataset — with the ‖Δμ‖ probe directions as the behavior vectors — would surface whatever behavioral clusters the DPO training installed, without needing to specify them in advance. That's the experiment after the current sweep completes.
